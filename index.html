Chapter 36: URI Drugs
Antihistamines · Decongestants · Antitussives · Expectorants — Lilley 10e
⚡ Quick Hits — Ch.36
  • OTC cough/cold products CONTRAINDICATED in children <2 yrs (FDA 2008)
  • URI drugs only RELIEVE symptoms — they do NOT eliminate the virus
  • Do NOT use antihistamines as SOLE therapy in acute asthma — use albuterol/epinephrine
  • Oxymetazoline (Afrin) max 3 days — longer causes rebound congestion addiction
  • Guaifenesin = ONLY expectorant with reflex GI stimulation mechanism
HIGH YIELDAntihistamines
▼
🔵 MechanismHistamine released from mast cells/basophils during allergic reactions binds to receptors → allergic S&S. H1 blockers compete for these receptors, preventing vasodilation, edema, secretions.

H1 Blockers — 1st Generation (Sedating, cross BBB)

  • Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) — highest sedation; AVOID in elderly (falls, anticholinergic, "hangover")
  • Chlorpheniramine — moderate sedation

H1 Blockers — 2nd Generation (Non-sedating, peripherally acting)

  • Fexofenadine (Allegra) — least sedating; does NOT cross BBB
  • Loratadine (Claritin) — desloratadine is active metabolite; OTC
  • Cetirizine (Zyrtec) — low sedation; OTC; now available injectable
⭐ MUST KNOW2nd-gen antihistamines are "peripherally acting" — do NOT readily cross blood-brain barrier → far fewer CNS effects. All available OTC. Stop ≥4 days before allergy testing.

H2 Blockers (GI only — NOT respiratory)

  • Cimetidine (Tagamet), Famotidine (Pepcid), Nizatidine (Axid) — hyperacidity/ulcer disease

Key Nursing Points

  • Antihistamines dry secretions → risk viscous mucus → atelectasis in lower respiratory disease
  • Paradoxical reactions in elderly: irritability, dizziness, confusion, sedation, hypotension
  • Give early in histamine reaction for best effect; take with meals; avoid alcohol
🔴 DangerNOT sole therapy in acute asthma attack. Use rapidly acting bronchodilator (albuterol) or epinephrine first.
🟣 Mnemonic — DICED (antihistamine effects)Drowsiness · Impaired reflexes · Constipation/dry mouth · Excitability (paradox in elderly) · Depress CNS
📋 Prof. NoteDiphenhydramine is now being misused for a potential "high." Excessive doses can cause serious consequences including death. Educate patients and monitor for misuse.

HIGH YIELDDecongestants
▼
🔵 MechanismAdrenergics constrict blood vessels in nasal mucosa → reduces swelling and pressure → membranes drain. Anticholinergics and intranasal steroids work via separate anti-inflammatory pathways.

Three Groups

  • Adrenergics (sympathomimetics): Ephedrine, Oxymetazoline (Afrin) — largest group
  • Anticholinergics (parasympatholytics): Less common
  • Intranasal Corticosteroids: Beclomethasone (Beconase), Budesonide (Rhinocort), Fluticasone (Flonase), Triamcinolone (Nasacort), Flunisolide (Nasalide), Ciclesonide (Omnaris)
🔴 Rebound Congestion — Oxymetazoline (Afrin)MAX 3 days topical use. Rapid absorption → rapid decline → patient redoses repeatedly → addiction cycle. Intranasal steroids and anticholinergics do NOT cause rebound congestion.

Adrenergic Contraindications

  • Narrow-angle glaucoma, uncontrolled CVD, hypertension, diabetes, hyperthyroidism
  • Prostatitis, BPH, history of CVA/TIA, cerebral arteriosclerosis, long-standing asthma
🟢 PearlIntranasal steroids target the inflammatory response — used prophylactically for chronic upper respiratory symptoms. No rebound congestion.
✓ OK
Antitussives (Cough Suppressants)
▼
🔵 MechanismOpioid (codeine, hydrocodone) and non-opioid (dextromethorphan) types act directly on the medullary cough center. Benzonatate anesthetizes stretch receptor cells in the respiratory tract.

Opioid Antitussives

  • Codeine and hydrocodone — only opioids approved for cough
  • Provide analgesia; drying effect on mucosa → ↑ secretion viscosity → reduces runny nose
  • SE: sedation, N/V, lightheadedness, constipation, CNS depression
  • ⚠️ DO NOT mix with alcohol — life-threatening CNS depression

Non-Opioid Antitussives

  • Dextromethorphan — most common OTC; same MOA, no opioid properties, no CNS depression; SE: dizziness, drowsiness, nausea
  • Benzonatate — anesthetizes stretch receptors; SE: dizziness, headache, sedation, nausea, constipation, pruritus
  • Diphenhydramine — also antitussive; SE: sedation, dry mouth, anticholinergic effects
🔴 SafetyMost antitussives cause sedation/drowsiness. Do NOT drive or operate heavy machinery. Codeine + alcohol = life-threatening CNS/respiratory depression.
✓ OK
Expectorants
▼
🔵 MechanismBreak down and thin respiratory secretions to facilitate expectoration. Two mechanisms: (1) Reflex stimulation via GI irritation — guaifenesin only. (2) Direct stimulation of secretory glands.

Guaifenesin (Mucinex) — Only Reflex-Acting Expectorant

  • Increases hydration of respiratory tract → maintains sol layer for ciliary clearance → reduces mucus viscosity
  • Used for: URI, bronchitis, laryngitis, pharyngitis, pertussis, influenza, measles, chronic paranasal sinusitis
  • Works best with adequate hydration — encourage 8+ glasses of water daily
🟢 PearlGuaifenesin is the ONLY expectorant using reflex stimulation via GI irritation. All other expectorants work by direct gland stimulation.
✓ OK
Chapter 37: Respiratory Drugs
Bronchodilators · LTRAs · Corticosteroids · Monoclonal Antibodies — Lilley 10e
⚡ Quick Hits — Ch.37
  • SABA = rescue (albuterol). LABA = maintenance only. LABA NEVER for acute attack.
  • Ipratropium/Tiotropium = maintenance COPD — NOT for acute bronchospasm
  • Montelukast: 2020 FDA Black Box Warning — serious mood/behavioral changes including suicidality
  • After inhaled corticosteroid: RINSE MOUTH to prevent oral candidiasis
  • Switching systemic → inhaled steroids: TAPER slowly — abrupt switch = Addisonian crisis = death risk
HIGH YIELDBeta-Adrenergic Agonists (SABA & LABA)
▼

SABAs — Short-Acting Beta Agonists (RESCUE)

  • Albuterol (Ventolin) — selective β₂; most common rescue
  • Levalbuterol (Xopenex) — selective β₂
  • Terbutaline (Brethine) — β₂ predominant
  • Metaproterenol (Alupent) — nonselective (β₁+β₂); more cardiac SE
⭐ MUST KNOWSABAs = rescue inhalers for ACUTE bronchospasm. Patients must use SABA as rescue treatment for acute symptoms. Do NOT use LABAs for acute attacks.

LABAs — Long-Acting Beta Agonists (MAINTENANCE)

  • Salmeterol (Serevent), Formoterol (Foradil, Perforomist), Arformoterol (Brovana)
  • Newer: Indacaterol (Arcapta Neohaler), Vilanterol+Fluticasone (Breo Ellipta), Olodaterol (Striverdi), Vilanterol+Umeclidinium (Anoro Ellipta)
🔴 CRITICALLABAs have LONGER onset — NEVER use for acute treatment. Always ensure patient has SABA for rescue.

Selectivity & Adverse Effects

  • Alpha/beta nonselective (Epinephrine): insomnia, restlessness, anorexia, cardiac stim, hyperglycemia, tremor, HA
  • Nonselective beta (Metaproterenol): cardiac stim, tremor, anginal pain, HA
  • Selective β₂ (Albuterol): hypo/hypertension, HA, tremor (fewer SE)
🟢 PearlAssess caffeine intake — caffeine has sympathomimetic effects and potentiates adverse effects of beta agonists. Beta agonist overdosage may be life-threatening — take exactly as prescribed.
✓ OK
Anticholinergics — Respiratory (Ipratropium, Tiotropium)
▼
🔵 MechanismBlock ACh receptors on bronchial tree → prevent bronchoconstriction → indirectly cause airway dilation. Also reduce secretions in COPD.
  • Ipratropium (Atrovent) — shorter acting; often combined with albuterol (DuoNeb)
  • Tiotropium (Spiriva) — once daily; long-acting
  • MAINTENANCE only — NOT for acute bronchospasm (slow/prolonged action)
  • HFA inhalers: NO longer contraindicated in peanut/soy allergy

Contraindications & Adverse Effects

  • Allergy to atropine; caution: narrow-angle glaucoma, prostate enlargement
  • SE: dry mouth/throat, nasal congestion, palpitations, GI distress, urinary retention, ↑ IOP, HA, coughing, anxiety
🟢 PearlAssess history of BPH, glaucoma, GI distress, and palpitations before giving anticholinergics — these conditions will be potentiated.
✓ OK
Xanthine Derivatives (Theophylline)
▼
🔵 MechanismInhibit phosphodiesterase → ↑ cAMP → bronchial smooth muscle relaxation. Also stimulate CNS medullary respiratory center → enhanced respiratory drive.
  • Natural: theophylline (metabolized to caffeine), theobromine, caffeine
  • Synthetic: aminophylline (metabolized to theophylline), dyphylline
  • Therapeutic range: 10–20 mcg/mL (narrow therapeutic index)
  • Now DE-EMPHASIZED in mild asthma — greater drug interactions, high interpatient variability

Adverse Effects

  • GI: nausea, vomiting, anorexia, gastroesophageal reflux
  • Cardiac: sinus tachycardia, extrasystole, palpitations, ventricular dysrhythmias
  • Toxicity (>20 mcg/mL): seizures, arrhythmias — assess cardiovascular status carefully
🔴 Narrow Therapeutic IndexMonitor serum levels. Assess caffeine intake — potentiates. Do not crush sustained-release tablets. Monitor HR, BP, cardiac history.
✓ OK
HIGH YIELDLeukotriene Receptor Antagonists (LTRAs)
▼
🔵 MechanismLeukotrienes cause inflammation, bronchoconstriction, mucus production → coughing, wheezing, SOB. LTRAs block leukotriene receptors on immune cells in lungs → reduce inflammation.
  • Montelukast (Singulair), Zafirlukast, Zileuton — oral administration
  • Prophylaxis and LONG-TERM treatment of asthma (≥12 years); NOT for acute attacks
  • Zafirlukast: headache, nausea, diarrhea, liver dysfunction; monitor LFTs
🔴 BLACK BOX WARNING — Montelukast (2020 FDA)Serious neuropsychiatric events including agitation, depression, suicidal thoughts and actions. Educate patient AND family. Monitor closely. Weigh risks vs benefits.
✓ OK
HIGH YIELDRespiratory Corticosteroids
▼
🔵 MechanismStabilize cell membranes that release bronchoconstricting substances. Dual effect: reduce inflammation AND enhance beta agonist activity. Restore bronchial smooth muscle β₂ responsiveness.

Inhaled (ICS)

  • Beclomethasone, Budesonide, Fluticasone, Flunisolide, Triamcinolone, Ciclesonide, Mometasone, Dexamethasone
  • Combination: Symbicort (budesonide+formoterol), Advair (fluticasone+salmeterol), Dulera (formoterol+mometasone)

Systemic (severe/acute)

  • Prednisone (oral), Methylprednisolone (IV or oral)
⭐ MUST KNOW — Rinse Mouth!After EVERY inhaled corticosteroid → rinse mouth and gargle water immediately → prevents oral candidiasis (thrush)
🔴 Addisonian Crisis RiskSwitching from systemic → inhaled steroids: TAPER slowly. Rapid switch without tapering = adrenal gland failure = death. Assess for adrenal disorders before initiating.
⭐ Pediatric WarningSystemic corticosteroids → bone growth suppression in children/adolescents. HPA axis suppression → growth stunting. Monitor growth.
✓ OK
Monoclonal Antibody Antiasthmatics
▼

  • Mepolizumab (Nucala) & Reslizumab (Cinqair) — IL-5 receptor antagonists
  • Dupilumab — IL-4 antagonist

HIGH YIELDInhaler Nursing Education
▼
⭐ MUST KNOW — Inhaler OrderGive bronchodilator (albuterol) FIRST → wait 3–5 minutes → THEN corticosteroid inhaler. The bronchodilator opens the airway so the steroid can penetrate deeper. After steroid inhaler → ALWAYS rinse mouth to prevent oral candidiasis.
🔵 Albuterol TachycardiaAlbuterol stimulates β₂ receptors but also has β₁ effect on the heart → tachycardia lasting approximately 30 minutes. This is expected and temporary. Reassure patient: "Your heart rate will be fast for about 30 minutes after your inhaler — that is normal."

Corticosteroid Inhaler — 3 S Side Effects

  • Salt: Fluid/sodium retention → weight gain, edema
  • Sugar: Hyperglycemia → monitor blood glucose, especially in diabetics
  • Sex hormone: Testosterone-like effect → mood changes, aggression, personality shifts
  • Always rinse and gargle mouth after ICS — prevents thrush (candidiasis)
  • MDIs: coordinate inhalation correctly — ~10% drug delivery to lungs
  • Wait 1–2 min between puffs of SAME drug; 2–5 min between DIFFERENT drugs
  • Capsule inhalers resemble oral capsules — FDA reports of patients swallowing them. Educate explicitly NOT to swallow.
  • Do NOT crush or chew oral sustained-release tablets; take with food to ↓ GI upset
  • Encourage journaling: peak flow levels, S&S, improvements, adverse effects
  • Therapeutic effects: ↓ dyspnea, wheezing, restlessness, anxiety; improved SpO₂, ABGs, activity tolerance, QOL

Asthma
Chronic inflammatory disorder — reversible airflow obstruction
⚡ Quick Hits — Asthma
  • Silent chest = absent breath sounds = NO air movement = PRE-ARREST emergency
  • Early attack ABG: Respiratory ALKALOSIS (hyperventilation blows off CO₂)
  • Late/severe ABG: Respiratory ACIDOSIS (fatigue → hypoventilation → CO₂ retention)
  • Normalizing PaCO₂ in EARLY attack = RED FLAG for impending respiratory failure, NOT improvement
  • Albuterol → tachycardia for ~30 min (beta-1 effect) — reassure patient it is expected and temporary
  • Inhaler order: Albuterol FIRST → wait 3–5 min → THEN steroid inhaler → rinse mouth
  • Steroid 3 S's: Salt (fluid retention/weight gain) · Sugar (hyperglycemia) · Sex hormone (aggression)
  • Avoid NSAIDs and beta-blockers — both trigger bronchoconstriction

  • Silent chest = PRE-ARREST

HIGH YIELDAsthma — Pathophysiology & Types
▼
🔵 DefinitionChronic inflammatory disorder of bronchi/bronchioles — recurrent REVERSIBLE shortness of breath from bronchospasm, mucosal edema, and viscous mucus production.

Types

  • Allergic/Extrinsic: Type I hypersensitivity — IgE-mediated. Elevated eosinophils. Triggers: dust mites, pollen, pet dander, mold, cockroach.
  • Intrinsic/Idiopathic: No specific allergen. Triggers: respiratory infections, stress, cold weather, exercise, smoking.
🔴 Drug Triggers to AVOID in AsthmaNSAIDs (ASA, ibuprofen, naproxen, ketorolac) — shunt arachidonic acid to leukotriene pathway → bronchoconstriction. Beta-blockers — block β₂ → unopposed bronchoconstriction. Even cardioselective beta-blockers carry risk in severe asthma.

Status Asthmaticus

  • Severe prolonged attack UNRESPONSIVE to standard bronchodilator therapy — MEDICAL EMERGENCY
  • Key sign: Pulsus paradoxus — SBP drop >10 mmHg during inhalation
  • Management: IV magnesium sulfate, continuous albuterol, systemic corticosteroids → intubation if unresponsive
✓ OK
HIGH YIELDAsthma — ABG Progression & PEFR Zones
▼

Early/Mild Attack — Respiratory Alkalosis

  • Hyperventilation → blows off CO₂
  • pH: ↑ >7.45 | PaCO₂: ↓ <35 mmHg | PaO₂: ↓ <80 mmHg

Late/Severe Attack — Respiratory Acidosis

  • Patient fatigues → hypoventilates → CO₂ retained
  • pH: ↓ <7.35 | PaCO₂: ↑ >45 mmHg | PaO₂: ↓ <80 mmHg
🔴 RED FLAG — Normalizing PaCO₂In an EARLY attack, a normalizing or rising PaCO₂ means the patient is FATIGUING and can no longer hyperventilate. This signals impending respiratory failure — NOT improvement. Escalate care immediately.

PEFR Action Zones

  • 🟢 Green >80% predicted — maintenance therapy; no acute intervention needed
  • 🟡 Yellow 50–80% — rescue albuterol Q4H for 1–2 days; contact provider
  • 🔴 Red <50% — IMMEDIATE intervention required; consider intubation

COPD
Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease — Pink Puffer vs. Blue Bloater
⚡ Quick Hits — COPD
  • Target SpO₂ for COPD: 88–93% — avoid over-oxygenation (suppresses hypoxic drive → apnea)
  • Pink Puffer = Emphysema (dyspnea, barrel chest, weight loss, pursed-lip breathing)
  • Blue Bloater = Chronic Bronchitis (productive cough AM, cyanosis, edema, crackles)
  • Chronic Bronchitis diagnosis: ≥3 months of symptoms × 2 consecutive years
  • Limit carbohydrates — carb metabolism RQ=1.0 (more CO₂ produced) vs fat RQ=0.7
  • Avoid dairy products — increases mucus production
  • Avoid caffeine — acts as diuretic, dehydrates secretions making them harder to clear
  • High protein diet promotes healing (avoid if renal or liver failure)
  • Emphysema: brittle fibrotic lungs — high-pressure ventilation → pneumothorax risk
  • COPD retains CO₂ because cannot fully exhale — patient rebreathes used air
  • Report elevated Hgb (polycythemia) immediately — ↑ blood viscosity → stroke/DVT/PE
COPD
🔬 Pathophysiology ▸
  • Chronic airflow obstruction
  • Air trapping → hyperinflation
  • Hypercapnia → respiratory acidosis
  • Emphysema: alveolar wall destruction, ↓ elasticity
  • Bronchitis: smooth muscle hypertrophy, mucus hypersecretion
😮 Signs & Symptoms ▸
  • Emphysema: dyspnea, barrel chest, pursed-lip breathing, weight loss, pink skin (Pink Puffer)
  • Bronchitis: productive cough (AM), cyanosis, edema, crackles/wheezes (Blue Bloater)
  • Both: tachypnea, accessory muscle use
🔭 Labs ▸
  • SpO₂ target 88–93%
  • PaCO₂ >45 (hypercapnia)
  • Polycythemia (compensatory)
  • HCO₃ elevated (renal compensation)
  • CXR: hyperinflation, flat diaphragm
🩺 Nursing ▸
  • High Fowler's / tripod
  • Titrate O₂ carefully (88–93%)
  • Pursed-lip breathing (2:4 ratio)
  • 2–3 L fluids/day (thin secretions)
  • Small frequent high-protein meals
💊 Medications ▸
  • SABA: Albuterol at onset of dyspnea
  • Anticholinergics: Ipratropium, Tiotropium
  • BiPAP (hypercapnic failure)
  • Guaifenesin (expectorant)
  • Roflumilast (Daliresp) — PDE4 inhibitor
⚠️ Complications ▸
  • Cor pulmonale (right HF)
  • Pneumothorax (emphysema blebs)
  • Polycythemia → stroke/DVT/PE
  • Frequent infections
📚 Education ▸
  • Smoking cessation (#1 intervention)
  • Flu vaccine annually; Pneumococcal Q4 yrs
  • Limit carbs (↑ CO₂ production)
  • Oral hygiene before meals
  • Report: ↑ sputum, new dyspnea, fever
HIGH YIELDCOPD — Pink Puffer vs. Blue Bloater
▼
🔵 Key Fact"Emphysema" and "Chronic Bronchitis" are no longer separate diagnoses — both are included under COPD. Smoking (#1 cause, >30 pack-year history typical). Also: occupational irritants, alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, repeated childhood infections.
Type 1 — "Pink Puffer" (Emphysema)
  • Alveolar wall destruction → ↓ elasticity
  • Air trapping → barrel chest
  • Pursed-lip breathing, tripod position
  • Dyspnea WITHOUT chronic cough
  • Weight loss/cachexia
  • Pink skin (compensatory hyperventilation)
  • Clubbed fingers (chronic hypoxia)
Type 2 — "Blue Bloater" (Chronic Bronchitis)
  • Chronic bronchial inflammation
  • Smooth muscle hypertrophy + mucus hypersecretion
  • Chronic productive cough (WORST in AM)
  • Cyanosis (V/Q mismatch)
  • Peripheral edema (cor pulmonale)
  • Crackles/wheezes on auscultation
  • Recurrent respiratory infections
🔴 Cor PulmonaleChronic hypoxia → pulmonary vasoconstriction → ↑ pulmonary pressure → RV failure. Signs: JVD, dependent pitting edema, sudden weight gain from fluid retention. Avoid opioids and benzodiazepines in COPD exacerbations — suppress respiratory drive.

Chronic Bronchitis — Diagnosis Criteria

  • Must have ≥3 consecutive months of productive cough for 2 consecutive years
  • Frequent pneumonia risk: retained secretions pool in alveoli → bacterial growth → infection
  • Avoid anticholinergics — dry secretions further → harder to cough up → ↑ pneumonia risk

Emphysema — Key Pathophysiology

  • Smoke/irritant → alveolar inflammation → scar tissue (fibrosis) → loss of surface area
  • Fibrotic lungs are brittle — high-pressure ventilation risks pneumothorax; use cautiously
  • Cannot fully exhale → CO₂ trapping → patient rebreathes used air → chronic hypercapnia
  • Barrel chest = air trapping from inability to exhale completely

COPD Patient Teaching — Diet

  • Avoid dairy products — increase mucus production
  • Avoid caffeine — diuretic effect dehydrates and thickens secretions
  • High protein diet promotes tissue healing (hold if renal or liver failure)
  • Small frequent meals — large meals elevate diaphragm and worsen dyspnea

Sleep Apnea (OSA)
Obstructive & Central Sleep Apnea — Diagnosis · Treatment · Perioperative Care
⚡ Quick Hits — OSA
  • OSA CANNOT be diagnosed clinically — requires polysomnography (sleep study)
  • AHI: Mild 5–14 | Moderate 15–29 | Severe ≥30 events/hour
  • CPAP: ≥4 hrs/night, ≥70% of nights — does NOT cure OSA, only works while worn
  • Nocturia in OSA = ANP release from micro-arousals (NOT from fluid intake)
  • Narcotics + OSA = #1 cause of respiratory arrest on surgical floors — monitor continuously
  • SpO₂ can drop to 70s before patient wakes — never leave OSA patient unmonitored on opioids
  • Perioperative: continuous SpO₂, CPAP at bedside, semi-Fowler's — AVOID supine
OSA
🔬 Pathophysiology ▸
  • Airway collapse during sleep despite effort
  • Repeated hypoxemia + arousal cycles
  • Sympathetic surges → HTN, tachycardia
  • Fragmented N3/REM → non-restorative sleep
  • Hypoxic vasoconstriction → pulmonary HTN
😮 Signs & Symptoms ▸
  • Loud snoring, witnessed apneas, gasping
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS)
  • Morning headaches (CO₂ retention)
  • Nocturia (ANP release from arousals)
  • Cognitive impairment, mood changes
🔭 Diagnostics ▸
  • Polysomnography (gold standard)
  • AHI: Mild 5–14 | Mod 15–29 | Severe ≥30
  • STOP-BANG screening (≥3 = high risk)
  • Home sleep test (less sensitive)
  • Epworth Sleepiness Scale
🩺 Nursing ▸
  • Semi-Fowler's HOB 30–45°
  • Continuous SpO₂ monitoring
  • Avoid supine positioning
  • CPAP at bedside (perioperative)
  • Notify anesthesia team preoperatively
💊 Treatment ▸
  • CPAP ≥4 hrs/night ≥70% nights
  • BiPAP for CPAP-intolerant
  • APAP (auto-titrating)
  • Weight loss (10% → ↓AHI significantly)
  • Positional therapy, oral appliances
⚠️ Complications ▸
  • Systemic + pulmonary HTN
  • Atrial fibrillation, heart failure
  • Type 2 diabetes (insulin resistance)
  • Stroke (nocturnal hypoxemia)
  • Post-op respiratory failure
📚 Education ▸
  • CPAP does NOT cure — only works while worn
  • Use every night including naps
  • Avoid alcohol, sedatives, opioids
  • Sleep on side (lateral positioning)
  • Weight loss goals if BMI elevated

HIGH YIELDOSA — Diagnosis & Treatment
▼

Polysomnography (Gold Standard)

  • Monitors: SpO₂, EEG (sleep staging), EMG, EOG, ECG, respiratory effort bands, nasal/oral airflow, snore mic, body position
  • AHI: Mild 5–14 | Moderate 15–29 | Severe ≥30
⭐ MUST KNOW — CPAP TeachingUse every night including naps. ≥4 hrs/night, ≥70% of nights. Does NOT cure OSA — only works while worn. Humidification reduces dryness/discomfort. Skipping even one night can worsen daytime symptoms.
  • BiPAP — separate inspiratory + expiratory pressures (for CPAP-intolerant patients)
  • APAP — auto-titrating pressure; adjusts breath-by-breath
  • Positional therapy — lateral (side) position; elevate HOB 30–45°
  • Weight loss: even 10% reduction significantly decreases AHI
  • AVOID: alcohol, opioids, benzodiazepines, sedating antihistamines (relax pharyngeal muscles, suppress arousal)
🔴 Perioperative PriorityOSA = significantly higher risk for post-op respiratory complications. Continuous SpO₂ monitoring, CPAP at bedside, semi-Fowler's positioning (HOB 30–45°). AVOID supine — worsens airway collapse. Notify anesthesia team.
🟢 HTN-OSA ConnectionHTN resistant to 3+ antihypertensives → screen for OSA. Repeated sympathetic surges from arousals → sustained vascular remodeling → persistent HTN. Treating OSA can lower BP.
🔴 Narcotics + OSA = Critical RiskOpioids cause CNS depression AND relax pharyngeal muscles — compounded risk. #1 cause of patient codes on surgical floors. SpO₂ can drop to low 70s before self-arousal. Continuous monitoring mandatory. If patient removes CPAP — reapply immediately.
📋 Prof. Note — Clinical RealityOSA patient on narcotics: you will see SpO₂ 95% awake → drops to 70s asleep. Tongue falls back, airway occludes, saturation plummets. Signs you are catching it early: dropping SpO₂, heart rate dropping, patient turning cyanotic/purplish. Call code, give Narcan, do compressions if pulseless. Patient later removes CPAP mask unmonitored → can code again. This is the #1 preventable in-hospital death on surgical floors.

Tuberculosis (TB)
Mycobacterium tuberculosis · RIPE Therapy · Airborne Precautions
⚡ Quick Hits — TB
  • RIPE = Rifampin + Isoniazid + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol — 6–12 months
  • 3 negative AFB sputum cultures on 3 SEPARATE days = clearance (remove isolation)
  • INH → ALWAYS supplement pyridoxine (B6) 25–50 mg daily (prevents neuropathy)
  • Rifampin → turns ALL body fluids orange-red (normal); OCP ineffective (CYP450 inducer)
  • Ethambutol → #1 toxicity = OPTIC NEURITIS — monitor vision monthly; not for young children
TUBERCULOSIS
🔬 Pathophysiology ▸
  • Airborne droplet nuclei inhalation
  • Macrophage engulfs but can't destroy M.TB
  • Granuloma formation (caseous necrosis)
  • Latent: contained. Active: replicating.
  • Reactivation: HIV, steroids, malnutrition
😮 Signs & Symptoms ▸
  • Chronic productive cough ≥3 weeks
  • Hemoptysis (cavitary disease)
  • Drenching night sweats
  • Weight loss, anorexia, fatigue
  • Low-grade fever, chills
🔭 Diagnostics ▸
  • TST (Mantoux) or IGRA (QuantiFERON)
  • Sputum AFB × 3 (separate days)
  • CXR: upper lobe infiltrates/cavitation
  • Sputum culture (gold standard)
  • Nucleic acid amplification test (NAAT)
🩺 Nursing ▸
  • Airborne precautions: N95, negative pressure
  • 3 negative AFB = clearance from isolation
  • Monitor LFTs monthly (RIPE all hepatotoxic)
  • Directly Observed Therapy (DOT)
  • Mandatorily reportable to public health
💊 RIPE Therapy ▸
  • Rifampin: RNA polymerase inhibitor, orange fluids
  • Isoniazid: mycolic acid inhibitor, B6 always
  • Pyrazinamide: intracellular, most hepatotoxic
  • Ethambutol: optic neuritis, monthly vision
  • 6–12 months total — NEVER stop early
⚠️ Complications ▸
  • MDR-TB (multidrug resistant)
  • XDR-TB (extensively drug resistant)
  • Drug-induced hepatitis (all RIPE drugs)
  • Miliary TB (hematogenous spread)
  • TB meningitis, pleural effusion
📚 Education ▸
  • Complete FULL course — partial = resistance
  • No alcohol (hepatotoxicity)
  • No acetaminophen with INH
  • Rifampin: orange fluids normal, use glasses
  • Report visual changes immediately (EMB)
HIGH YIELDTB — Pathophysiology & Transmission
▼
🔵 Pathophysiology Steps1. Inhale airborne droplet nuclei → alveolar deposit
2. Macrophage phagocytosis → M. TB survives (resists phagolysosomal fusion)
3. Adaptive immunity: CD4+ helpers, CD8+ killers, B cells produce antibodies
4. Granuloma formation (caseous necrosis center) — walls off bacteria

Latent vs. Active TB

  • Latent: Bacteria contained; asymptomatic; NON-infectious; TST positive
  • Active: Granuloma fails; bacteria replicate; symptomatic; INFECTIOUS
  • Reactivation: HIV (#1 risk, 20–30× increased risk), steroids, TNF inhibitors, malnutrition

Classic Presentation

  • Chronic productive cough ≥3 weeks + hemoptysis (late/serious = cavitary disease)
  • Night sweats (drenching, recurrent)
  • Unexplained weight loss, anorexia, fatigue
  • Low-grade fever and chills
🟣 Mnemonic — Classic TB TriadCAWN — Cough (chronic ≥3 wks) · A(n)orexia/weight loss · (night) sweats · Nocturn fevers
🔴 Airborne Isolation PrecautionsNegative pressure room, N95 respirator (fit-tested) for all HCW, surgical mask on patient during transport, door closed at ALL times. Continue UNTIL 3 negative AFB cultures confirmed.
📋 Public HealthTB is MANDATORILY REPORTABLE. Report all confirmed AND suspected cases to local health department within 24 hours. Contact tracing initiated immediately. MDR-TB and XDR-TB = public health emergencies.

HIGH YIELDRIPE Therapy — TB Medications
▼
⭐ MUST KNOWAll 4 RIPE drugs used together in intensive phase (first 2 months). Rifampin + Isoniazid continue in continuation phase (4–7 more months). Total: 6–12 months. Incomplete treatment = #1 cause of drug-resistant TB.

R — Rifampin (Bactericidal)

  • MOA: Inhibits bacterial RNA polymerase
  • ⚠️ ALL body fluids turn orange-red — urine, sweat, tears, saliva — NORMAL, warn family
  • ⚠️ Soft contact lenses will be permanently stained — use glasses
  • ⚠️ OCP become INEFFECTIVE — potent CYP450 inducer — use backup contraception
  • Monitor LFTs; strong drug interactions (warfarin, antiretrovirals, antifungals)

I — Isoniazid/INH (Bactericidal)

  • MOA: Inhibits mycolic acid synthesis in cell wall
  • ⭐ ALWAYS supplement pyridoxine (B6) 25–50 mg daily — prevents neuropathy — NOT optional
  • Monitor: ataxia, numbness/tingling in hands/feet — report immediately
  • NO alcohol (↑↑ hepatotoxicity), NO acetaminophen (additive hepatotoxicity)

P — Pyrazinamide/PZA (Bactericidal)

  • MOA: Targets semi-dormant intracellular bacteria in macrophage acid environment
  • MOST hepatotoxic of the four — monitor LFTs closely
  • Hyperuricemia → monitor for gout (joint pain, swelling)
  • Photosensitivity — use sunscreen

E — Ethambutol/EMB (Bacteriostatic)

  • MOA: Inhibits arabinosyl transferase → disrupts cell wall; prevents resistance
  • Primary toxicity: OPTIC NEURITIS — blurred vision, loss of red-green color discrimination, ↓ visual acuity
  • Baseline visual acuity BEFORE starting; repeat MONTHLY
  • Report any visual changes immediately — usually reversible if caught early
  • NOT recommended for young children (cannot reliably report visual changes)
🔴 ALL RIPE Drugs = HepatotoxicHold if AST/ALT >3× ULN (symptomatic) or >5× ULN (asymptomatic). Signs: jaundice, dark urine, fatigue, RUQ pain, N/V, light-colored stools. Monitor LFTs monthly. NO alcohol. NO acetaminophen.

Acid-Base Imbalances & ABG
Normal Values · 4 Imbalances · Compensation · Step-by-Step Reading
⚡ Quick Hits — ABG
  • Normal: pH 7.35–7.45 | PaCO₂ 35–45 | PaO₂ 80–100 | HCO₃ 22–26
  • CO₂ = respiratory acid | HCO₃ = metabolic base
  • ROME: Respiratory Opposite (pH↑=CO₂↓) | Metabolic Equal (pH↑=HCO₃↑)
  • Kussmaul breathing = deep rapid respirations = metabolic acidosis compensation (blowing off CO₂)
  • Compensation = the other system tries to normalize pH (lungs compensate metabolic disorders; kidneys compensate respiratory disorders)

Step-by-Step ABG Interpretation

  • Step 1: Look at pH — <7.35 = acidosis, >7.45 = alkalosis
  • Step 2: Look at PaCO₂ — if matches pH direction = respiratory cause
  • Step 3: Look at HCO₃ — if matches pH direction = metabolic cause
  • Step 4: Determine primary disorder
  • Step 5: Is the other system compensating? (moving opposite to primary)

HIGH YIELDFour Acid-Base Imbalances
▼
Respiratory Acidosis↑ CO₂ (hypoventilation)
pH <7.35, PaCO₂ >45
Causes: COPD, narcotic OD, NM disease, pneumonia, airway obstruction
S&S: ↓ LOC, HA, tremors, cyanosis
Compensate: kidneys retain HCO₃
Respiratory Alkalosis↓ CO₂ (hyperventilation)
pH >7.45, PaCO₂ <35
Causes: anxiety, PE, fever, pain, early asthma
S&S: dizziness, tingling, tetany, lightheadedness
Compensate: kidneys excrete HCO₃
Metabolic Acidosis↓ HCO₃ (gain acid or lose base)
pH <7.35, HCO₃ <22
Causes: DKA, renal failure, diarrhea, lactic acidosis
S&S: Kussmaul breathing, nausea, cerebral vasodilation
Compensate: lungs hyperventilate (↓ CO₂)
Metabolic Alkalosis↑ HCO₃ (lose acid or gain base)
pH >7.45, HCO₃ >26
Causes: vomiting, NG suction, diuretics, antacids, hypokalemia
S&S: slow respiration, restlessness, arrhythmias, diarrhea
Compensate: lungs hypoventilate (↑ CO₂)

Group II: pH=7.30, PaCO₂=60, HCO₃=24 → Respiratory Acidosis (uncompensated)
Group III: pH=7.50, PaCO₂=20, HCO₃=22 → Respiratory Alkalosis (uncompensated)
Group IV: pH=7.48, PaCO₂=40, HCO₃=28 → Metabolic Alkalosis (uncompensated)
